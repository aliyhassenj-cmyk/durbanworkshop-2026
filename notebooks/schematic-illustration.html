What is metadata in genomic epidemiology?

Metadata is the “data about the data” — the contextual information that turns a collection of genomes into an epidemiological story. Without metadata, a phylogenetic tree just shows how sequences are related. With metadata, you can ask:

  • Where did this transmission happen?
  • Who infected whom?
  • What patient characteristics predict spread?
  • Is the outbreak growing or shrinking?

This diagram maps the major types of metadata, the analytic approaches that use them, and the insights they produce.

Detailed guide to each section

📦 Metadata types (what you collect)

Metadata type Examples Why it matters
Demographics Age, sex, occupation, ethnicity Describes who is affected; helps adjust for confounding
Clinical Symptoms, severity score, comorbidities, hospitalisation, outcome Links genotype to disease phenotype
Temporal Date of symptom onset, date of sampling, date of reporting Enables time-scaled phylogenies and Rₜ estimation
Spatial GPS coordinate, administrative region, health facility Reveals geographic spread and clustering
Exposure Travel history, contact tracing, occupation, diet, animal contact Identifies risk factors and transmission routes

🔬 Analytic approaches (what you do with it)

Approach What it does Metadata it uses Key tools
Phylogenetic trait mapping Maps discrete or continuous traits (location, phenotype) onto the tree to infer ancestral states and associations Demographics, clinical, spatial phytools, ggtree, ape
Bayesian phylodynamics Co-estimates tree, time scale, and population dynamics from sequence + date data Temporal (dates) BEAST2, bdsky, phydynR
Transmission network inference Infers who-infected-whom using SNP distances + epi links (time, place) Temporal, spatial, exposure outbreaker2, TransPhylo, SeqTrack
Risk factor analysis Tests association between metadata variables and cluster membership or outcome Clinical, demographics, exposure glm(), lme4, gtsummary
Spatial epidemiology Detects spatial clusters, maps diffusion, tests for spatial association Spatial (coordinates, regions) spdep, DCluster, kriging
Machine learning Predicts outbreak risk, classifies lineages, identifies important features from mixed data All metadata types ranger, xgboost, tidymodels

📊 Outputs and insights

Output What it tells you
Descriptive summaries Table 1 stratified by cluster or lineage — who is in each transmission group?
Transmission parameters Is the outbreak growing? (R₀, Rₜ, growth rate, doubling time)
Spatial & temporal maps Where and when is transmission happening? Are cases clustering in space-time?
Transmission networks Who infected whom? How many unsampled cases exist?
Public health insights Who should be targeted? Where should interventions go? Is one group at higher risk?

Key principle: metadata adds the “where, when, and why” to the “what”

A phylogenetic tree alone tells you which strains are related. Adding metadata turns it into an epidemiological investigation:

Genomes + dates → when did the outbreak start? How fast is it growing?

Genomes + locations → where did it come from? Where is it spreading?

Genomes + exposures → what behaviours or contacts drive transmission?

Genomes + clinical data → does this strain cause more severe disease?
